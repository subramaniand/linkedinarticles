The opportunity

Why each section is structured this way
The headline number
Lead with the gross annual benefit, not the cost. CFOs anchor on the first number they see. "£11.8M annual value" frames the investment that follows as small, not large.
Avoid: leading with cost or technology details
The four KPI tiles
ROI, payback, benefit, and cost — the exact four metrics a CFO uses to evaluate any capital allocation. Every number must have a source footnote or it will be challenged in the room.
Avoid: NPV without IRR, or vice versa
Opportunity framing
The left panel makes inaction feel costly. The competitive framing is critical — CFOs respond to external benchmarks and the fear of falling behind sector peers more than internal efficiency arguments.
Avoid: technology jargon (LLMs, tokens, RAG)
Audited value sources
Split benefits into distinct, independently verifiable lines. "Productivity uplift" and "process automation" are defensible categories. Bundle them and the CFO will halve your number as a credibility discount.
Avoid: single blended benefit number
The conditional ask
This is the highest-value sentence on the slide. Offering staged funding with kill criteria dramatically reduces perceived risk and almost always accelerates approval. It signals confidence in the plan.
Avoid: asking for 3-year budget in one vote
Appendix signal
The footer tells the CFO that every number is auditable without cluttering the slide. Detail belongs in the appendix — referenced, not presented. A CFO who wants to dig will ask; most won't on a first read.
Avoid: financial model tables on the summary slide
